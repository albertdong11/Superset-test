## Q1: Comprehensive Investment Dossier — Sandstone (sandstone.com)

### Company Overview

**Sandstone** is an AI-native workflow platform purpose-built for in-house legal teams. Founded in 2025 and publicly launched in January 2026, the company is headquartered in Brooklyn, NY. It raised a **$10M seed round led by Sequoia Capital** with participation from SV Angel, Kearny Jackson, Webb Investment Network (Maynard Webb), Mantis VC (The Chainsmokers), Antiportfolio Ventures, Daybreak Ventures, and 20+ general counsel from companies including eBay and Bilt, plus founders/executives from Okta, Sun Microsystems, Autodesk, and Flatiron Health.

**Sequoia partner Bogomil Balkansky** led the investment. Notably, Balkansky had drafted a legal tech product requirements document in 2019 while at Google based on similar pain points he observed at VMware — meaning Sequoia had been thesis-driven on this space for years before Sandstone appeared.

---

### The Product

Sandstone positions itself as the **"operating system for in-house legal departments"** — a central hub that replaces the patchwork of spreadsheets, email, Slack threads, and disconnected tools that legal teams currently use.

**Core capabilities:**
- **Intake & triage automation** — AI agents classify incoming requests (sales agreements, employment contracts, NDAs, etc.) and route them appropriately
- **Context aggregation** — Pulls business context from Salesforce, HRIS, email, Slack, and other systems to arm legal with the "why" behind each request
- **Playbook-driven drafting** — Applies company-specific contract playbooks to start drafting agreements automatically
- **Institutional knowledge capture** — Learns from a team's decisions, precedents, and preferences to build dynamic, evolving playbooks ("context in motion")
- **Agent deployment in <10 minutes** — Legal teams can deploy their own AI agents without engineering help
- **Multi-LLM architecture** — Integrates OpenAI GPT and Anthropic Claude

**Key insight:** Sandstone doesn't just do contract review (there are dozens of those tools). It's a **workflow orchestration layer** that sits on top of existing systems and turns the legal department from a reactive bottleneck into a proactive function. The vision is that legal teams "open Sandstone first thing in the morning" the way a sales team opens Salesforce.

### Market Opportunity

**Legal AI market sizing:**
- **2025**: ~$2.4B globally
- **2026**: ~$2.7B (estimated)
- **2035**: ~$7.4B (projected, 13.1% CAGR)
- Legal tech spending surging 9.7% YoY as firms race to integrate AI

**The in-house legal TAM is specifically compelling:**
- ~100,000+ in-house legal departments in the US alone
- Average in-house legal spend is growing faster than revenue at most enterprises
- In-house teams are chronically understaffed — they handle 5-10x more requests than a decade ago with only marginally larger teams
- The pain is acute: spreadsheets as databases, manual intake via email/Slack, no institutional knowledge capture, massive context-switching overhead

**Why now:**
- LLMs finally make "text in, text out" legal work automatable at quality
- Law is arguably the single best vertical for LLMs — it's entirely language-based, high-value, and historically resistant to automation
- In-house teams (unlike law firms) are cost centers with budget pressure to do more with less — they have strong incentive to adopt
- 2026 is widely described as the "breakthrough year" for legal AI adoption

### Competitive Landscape

| Company | Focus | Funding | Differentiation |
|---------|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Harvey** | AI for law firms (Am Law 100) | $300M+ raised, ~$3B valuation | Research & multi-practice workflows for elite firms. Different buyer (law firm partners vs. in-house GCs). |
| **Ironclad** | Contract lifecycle management (CLM) | $333M raised | End-to-end contract management. Enterprise incumbent. Adding AI on top of existing CLM. |
| **Robin AI** | AI contract review + managed services | $56M raised | Clause-level automation for standardized agreements. Narrower scope. |
| **Crosby** | AI-enabled law firm | Sequoia-backed | Alternative legal services model. |
| **GC AI** | AI for in-house teams | Earlier stage | Most direct competitor. Less information available. |
| **Spellbook** | AI contract drafting | $30M+ raised | Focused on contract drafting specifically. |
| **Streamline AI** | Legal intake & triage | Growth stage | Workflow-focused competitor, less AI-native. |

**Sandstone's positioning:** They aren't competing head-to-head with Harvey (different buyer — law firms vs. in-house). The closest competitive threat is from CLM platforms like Ironclad adding AI features, or from point solutions like Spellbook expanding scope. Sandstone's bet is that the market wants a **new, AI-native system of record** for in-house teams rather than AI bolted onto legacy CLM.

### Bull Case

1. **Category-defining platform.** If Sandstone becomes the "Salesforce for legal," the TAM expands far beyond legal AI into legal operations software broadly (~$25B+). Every company with 5+ lawyers needs this.
2. **Sequoia conviction is real.** Balkansky had a thesis on this space for 5+ years. This isn't a spray-and-pray bet — it's a deeply researched conviction investment.
3. **Wedge → platform play.** Start with intake/triage (immediate pain, fast time-to-value), expand into contract drafting, matter management, spend management, compliance, and eventually become the legal ERP.
4. **Network effects potential.** As more companies use Sandstone, anonymized playbook data and contract benchmarks create a data moat that improves the product for everyone.
5. **Massive labor arbitrage.** In-house lawyers cost $200-400K fully loaded. If Sandstone saves a 5-person team even 1 FTE of work, the ROI is 10-20x the software cost.
6. **GC investors = distribution.** Having 20+ GCs as investors means built-in customer pipeline and credibility with the buyer persona.
7. **Timing is perfect.** 2026 is the inflection year for legal AI adoption. First-mover in "AI-native in-house platform" could be durable.

**Bull case valuation:** If they achieve $50-100M ARR in 4-5 years, a 20-30x revenue multiple gets you to a $1-3B outcome. Harvey's trajectory ($300M+ raised, ~$3B valuation) shows legal AI can command premium multiples.

---

### Bear Case

1. **First-time founders, young team.** Building enterprise software for GCs (conservative, risk-averse buyers) is hard. McKinsey teaches you to diagnose problems, not to sell software, manage churn, or build engineering orgs.
2. **Crowded and heating up fast.** Harvey, Ironclad, Robin AI, Spellbook, Streamline, GC AI — plus every CLM vendor is adding AI. There will be significant feature convergence.
3. **Enterprise sales cycles are brutal.** Legal departments at Fortune 500s take 6-12 months to buy. Small legal teams have small budgets. The "couple dozen" customers after months in market is early but not explosive.
4. **LLM commoditization risk.** If the core value is "apply LLM to legal text," that's increasingly table-stakes. Every competitor has access to the same foundation models. The moat must come from workflow, data, and switching costs — not the AI itself.
5. **Hallucination liability.** One bad AI-generated contract clause that costs a customer money could be devastating for trust and create legal liability for Sandstone itself.
6. **Incumbent response.** Ironclad ($333M raised) can add AI agents to their existing CLM with a much larger installed base. Thomson Reuters, LexisNexis could enter. Microsoft Copilot could build legal workflows.
7. **Retention risk in vertical SaaS.** If Sandstone is "nice to have" rather than "must have," churn will kill them. The product needs to become deeply embedded in daily workflows quickly.

**Bear case valuation:** If they struggle with enterprise sales and feature commoditization, this could plateau at $5-15M ARR and become an acqui-hire or modest exit ($50-150M).

---

### Key Risks (Ranked)

1. **Execution risk** — Can a 25-year-old first-time CEO build and scale an enterprise sales org targeting conservative legal buyers?
2. **Competitive convergence** — Will the in-house legal AI market consolidate around 1-2 winners, and can Sandstone win that race against better-funded incumbents?
3. **Product-market fit depth** — Are "couple dozen" customers using the product daily, or is it shelfware? Retention metrics are unknown.
4. **AI reliability** — Legal is zero-error-tolerance. One hallucinated clause could torpedo the company's reputation.
5. **Market timing** — If legal AI adoption is slower than expected (lawyers are famously conservative), burn rate could become an issue before the market matures.

---

### Investment Verdict

**Rating: Cautiously Bullish**

**What I like:**
- Sequoia conviction with a thesis-driven partner (Balkansky)
- Exceptional domain expertise on the team (lawyers who code)
- Right market, right timing — legal is the ideal LLM vertical
- Smart positioning: not competing with Harvey (law firms), carving out in-house
- GC investor base = built-in distribution and credibility
- "Operating system" ambition with a clear wedge (intake/triage)

**What concerns me:**
- Very early traction ("couple dozen" customers, mix of SMB and F500)
- First-time, young founders navigating enterprise sales
- $10M seed is modest for enterprise — will need to raise Series A within 12-18 months
- No disclosed metrics (ARR, NRR, usage data)
- Competitive landscape is dense and well-funded

**Bottom line:** This is a high-conviction seed bet on a great team in a massive, underserved market at the right time. The Sequoia backing and GC investor network give it disproportionate advantages at this stage. The key question is whether Fleisher can evolve from McKinsey consultant to enterprise CEO fast enough. If the next 12 months show strong NRR (>130%), expanding deal sizes, and a clear path to $3-5M ARR, the Series A will be highly competitive. If traction stalls, the crowded market will make fundraising harder.

**I'd invest at seed** if I could get allocation alongside Sequoia, knowing this is a 3-5 year bet on the team's ability to execute in a category that's clearly going to be large.
